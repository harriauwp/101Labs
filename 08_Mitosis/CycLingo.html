DNA Naming Expert

Navigate through the cell cycle phases to understand DNA states

Interphase

G1 (Gap 1) Phase

DNA Status: Chromatin (Unreplicated)

DNA exists as long, thin fibers called chromatin. It is not yet condensed into visible chromosomes. The cell contains a normal diploid amount of DNA (2n).

  • Cell grows in size
  • Organelles are duplicated
  • Synthesis of proteins required for DNA replication
  • Cell performs normal metabolic functions
Interphase

S (Synthesis) Phase

DNA Status: Replicating Chromatin

DNA replication occurs. By the end of this phase, the amount of DNA in the cell has doubled (2n → 4n), but it remains in the chromatin form (not condensed).

  • DNA replication takes place
  • Centrosomes are duplicated
  • Precise copying of the genetic material
  • Checkpoint ensures DNA is undamaged before replication
Interphase

G2 (Gap 2) Phase

DNA Status: Chromatin (Replicated)

The cell contains double the amount of DNA (4n). The DNA is still in chromatin form but is being checked for replication errors before mitosis begins.

  • Rapid cell growth and protein synthesis
  • Preparation for mitosis (cell division)
  • DNA damage checkpoints (G2 checkpoint)
  • Cytoskeleton prepares to move chromosomes
Mitosis

Prophase

DNA Status: Condensing into Chromosomes

Chromatin fibers condense into distinct, visible chromosomes. Each chromosome consists of two identical sister chromatids joined at the centromere.

  • Chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes
  • Nuclear envelope begins to break down
  • Nucleolus disappears
  • Spindle fibers begin to form from centrosomes
Mitosis

Metaphase

DNA Status: Aligned Chromosomes

Condensed chromosomes align along the metaphase plate (center of the cell). This ensures equal separation in the next step.

  • Chromosomes align at the cell's equator
  • Spindle fibers attach to kinetochores at centromeres
  • M Checkpoint ensures all chromosomes are attached
  • Maximum condensation of chromosomes
Mitosis

Anaphase

DNA Status: Separating Sister Chromatids

Sister chromatids split apart and are pulled toward opposite poles. Once separated, they are considered individual chromosomes.

  • Centromeres split
  • Sister chromatids are pulled apart
  • Cell elongates
  • Equal distribution of genetic material
Mitosis

Telophase

DNA Status: De-condensing

Chromosomes arrive at opposite poles and begin to de-condense back into chromatin. Two new nuclear envelopes form.

  • Nuclear envelopes reform around two sets of DNA
  • Chromosomes uncoil into chromatin
  • Spindle fibers break down
  • Mitosis is technically complete
Cell Division

Cytokinesis

DNA Status: Chromatin (2 Daughter Cells)

The cytoplasm divides, resulting in two distinct daughter cells. Each cell has an identical set of DNA in chromatin form (2n).

  • Physical division of the cytoplasm
  • Animal cells: Cleavage furrow forms
  • Plant cells: Cell plate forms
  • Returns to G1 phase
